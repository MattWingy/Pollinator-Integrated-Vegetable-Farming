# From Vegetables to Pollinators: How Farmers Can Cultivate Both Crops and Their Essential Partners

## Executive Summary

The answer to whether vegetable farmers can also become farmers of pollinators is a resounding yes. In fact, the integration of pollinator management into vegetable farming operations represents not only a viable diversification strategy but an increasingly essential practice for sustainable agriculture. Vegetable farmers are uniquely positioned to benefit from pollinator farming through multiple pathways: establishing managed beehives for honey production and pollination services, creating and maintaining pollinator habitats that support wild bee populations, or implementing integrated crop pollination systems that combine both approaches. Research demonstrates that fields with adequate pollinator populations can experience yield increases of 25-30%, while beekeeping operations can generate $300-600 per hive annually from honey sales alone. Beyond direct economic returns, farmers who cultivate pollinators benefit from improved crop quality, reduced dependency on commercial pollination services, and enhanced ecosystem resilience.

## The Critical Relationship Between Vegetable Production and Pollination

### Pollination Dependency of Vegetable Crops

The relationship between vegetable farming and pollinators extends far beyond simple coexistence. Animal pollinators, particularly bees, play an indispensable role in the successful production of numerous vegetable crops. Fruiting vegetables that require pollination from bees include cucumbers, gourds, melons, pumpkins, and squash—crops that fundamentally depend on pollination to produce fruit. When bees collect pollen and nectar from blossoms, they simultaneously move pollen between male and female flowers, facilitating the fertilization process that leads to fruit development. This mutually beneficial relationship sees bees gathering essential nutrients while crops receive the pollination services necessary for reproduction and yield.

The economic magnitude of this relationship cannot be overstated. **Globally, animal pollination contributes an estimated $235 to $577 billion annually to agriculture**. In the United States alone, pollinators contribute approximately $20 billion in crop production value each year, with insect pollination services adding more than $34 billion annually. Within the European Union, pollination services are valued at approximately €15 billion per year, with 78% of native flora and 84% of crops being either partially or fully dependent on insects for pollination. These figures underscore the fundamental importance of pollinators to global food security and agricultural economies.

### Impact on Crop Yield and Quality

The presence of adequate pollinator populations directly influences both the quantity and quality of vegetable production. Research has demonstrated that successful pollination improves multiple dimensions of crop output. A comprehensive meta-analysis of 190 studies revealed that **animal pollination improves food quality by approximately 23%**, with particularly strong effects on organoleptic traits such as size, shape, and commercial grade, which improved by 27%. Fields with abundant bee populations exhibit larger and more uniform fruits, with studies showing that sufficient bee populations can lead to crop yield increases of up to 30%. For specific crops, the impacts are even more pronounced: cherry weight can increase by 2.8% when pollinated by bees, and watermelon weight and quantity per plot show significant improvements with adequate animal pollination.

The quality improvements extend beyond mere size. Optimal pollination promotes fruit development through phytohormonal processes activated by successful fertilization, leading to simultaneous seed formation and fruit growth. This results in not only larger produce but also better-formed vegetables that command higher prices in the market. For many crops, large quantities of pollen must be delivered to female flowers to set marketable fruit, and cross-pollination often delivers larger individual fruits with better commercial characteristics. These quality enhancements have multiple positive economic and environmental consequences, making the safeguarding of pollination services essential to maintain food security.

## Pathways to Becoming a Pollinator Farmer

### Managed Beekeeping Integration

The most direct pathway for vegetable farmers to become pollinator farmers is through establishing managed beekeeping operations. Beekeeping offers multiple revenue streams while simultaneously supporting crop pollination. Commercial beekeepers can earn income through honey production, with each hive producing variable amounts depending on management and environmental conditions. **On average, beekeepers can make between $300 to $600 per hive annually from honey sales**, with some operations achieving significantly higher returns through premium pricing and diversified products. Beyond honey, beekeeping provides access to multiple marketable products including beeswax, pollen, propolis, and royal jelly, all of which command premium prices in natural product markets.

Managed beekeeping encompasses several distinct approaches suitable for different farm scales and objectives. Honey bees remain the most common managed pollinator, with colonies containing approximately 30,000-40,000 workers in spring, making them highly effective for supplementing pollination when wild pollinator populations are low. Commercial beekeeping operations with 300 or more hives can be very profitable, with many beekeepers migrating colonies to provide pollination services to farmers while simultaneously providing bees with abundant nectar sources for honey production. For smaller operations, even five or six hives can provide sufficient honey for home use and local sales.

#### Alternative Managed Pollinators

Alternative managed pollinators offer complementary options to honey bees:

- **Bumble bees**: Colonies of 50-300 individuals demonstrate extreme efficiency as pollinators due to characteristics including long tongues in some species, ability to fly in cold weather and low light, and capacity for buzz pollination essential for crops like tomatoes. Bumble bees are the primary pollinators used for most greenhouse tomatoes, producing larger and more even fruits through their specialized pollination technique.

- **Mason bees** (also called blue orchard bees): Only 250 female orchard mason bees are required to effectively pollinate an acre of apples, a task that would need 1.5 to 2 honey bee hives representing approximately 15,000 to 20,000 bees. These solitary bees are extremely efficient pollinators for orchards and spring fruits.

- **Wild native bees**: Support diverse crops year-round when provided with appropriate habitat. Management is habitat-based rather than colony-based, requiring establishment of nesting sites and food resources.

### Pollinator Habitat Creation and Management

Creating and managing pollinator habitat represents an equally viable pathway for vegetable farmers to support pollinator populations while enhancing their farming operations. This approach focuses on establishing and maintaining areas that provide essential resources for wild pollinators, including food sources, nesting sites, and hibernation locations. Research from the Farming with Alternative Pollinators (FAP) approach indicates that **by dedicating around 25% of their land to pollinator habitats, farmers can attract wild pollinators, improving biodiversity, increasing productivity, and naturally reducing pest populations**. Studies suggest that if 20 to 30 percent of the surrounding landscape within a mile of the farm is maintained as permanent pollen and nectar-rich habitat, many types of crops can get their pollination needs met from wild bees sustained by that habitat.

Effective pollinator habitat encompasses multiple landscape elements strategically positioned throughout agricultural land:

- **Flower-rich semi-natural permanent grasslands**: Key habitat and food source for pollinators such as bumblebees, solitary bees, butterflies, moths, and flies
- **Field margins and buffer strips**: Provide diverse floral resources and nesting sites for wild bees
- **Hedgerows**: Serve as corridors that bring pollinators from natural areas to farm fields
- **Wildflower strips**: Dedicated areas with diverse native flowering plants
- **Uncultivated land**: Significantly increases pollinator abundance when surrounding crop fields

### Integrated Crop Pollination Systems

Integrated Crop Pollination (ICP) represents the most comprehensive approach to pollinator farming, combining multiple pollinator species, habitat augmentation, and crop management practices to provide reliable and economical pollination. ICP is defined as "the combined use of multiple pollinator species, habitat augmentation, and crop management practices to provide reliable and economical pollination of crops." This approach recognizes that no single strategy will be the best option for all farming situations and that combining managed and wild pollinators with farm practices that support pollinator health creates more resilient and effective pollination systems.

#### Components of ICP

1. **Use of managed pollinator species**: May include honey bees, alternative managed bees like bumble bees and mason bees, and various types of wild bees, selected based on crop requirements and local conditions

2. **Habitat augmentation**: Adding floral and nesting resources to farms through mechanisms such as wildflower strips, meadows, and hedgerows that provide year-round support for pollinator populations

3. **Crop management practices**: Modifying pest management practices to reduce risks to pollinators, using conservation tillage, and allowing cover crops to bloom

A case study from Cory Klehm's farm demonstrated how integrating cover crops with varying blooming dates and high nutritional content significantly improved bee colony health, with the farmer attributing the bees staying on the farm to **a 30 percent increase in crop yield, with tomatoes seeing the biggest benefit**.

## Economic Models and Business Opportunities

### Direct Revenue from Beekeeping Products

Beekeeping provides vegetable farmers with multiple direct revenue streams that can significantly contribute to farm income. Honey production remains the primary product, with market demand consistently strong for natural, locally produced raw honey. Production costs for honey are relatively low compared to potential returns, and the product commands premium prices in many markets.

#### Income Potential

- **Small-scale operations** (5-10 hives): $1,000 to $10,000 annually
- **Medium-scale operations**: Supplemental to significant income
- **Commercial operations** (300+ hives): Full-time livelihood potential
- **Case study (Vietnam)**: Beekeeper with 500 stingless bee colonies earning approximately $3,800 USD per year, with honey selling for $75.90–$94.87 USD per liter

#### Diversified Bee Products

- **Honey**: Primary revenue source
- **Beeswax**: Used in cosmetics, candles, and artisan products
- **Propolis**: Valued in natural medicine and wellness products
- **Royal jelly**: Premium health product
- **Pollen**: Nutritional supplement
- **Bee sales**: Starter hives and packages for other beekeepers
- **Education services**: Training and consulting

### Pollination Services as a Revenue Stream

Renting beehives for pollination services represents a lucrative business opportunity that some beekeepers pursue as their primary income source. Pollination services involve temporarily placing bee colonies on farms during crop flowering periods to ensure adequate pollination. Some beekeepers earn a full-time income from pollination services alone, managing hundreds of beehives at a time for large farms. Commercial beekeeping operations often migrate their colonies across regions to provide pollination services to farmers, following crop blooming seasons throughout the year.

**Economic benefits for farmers:**
- Establishing natural pollinator habitats reduces farmers' need for rented commercial hives
- Saves the expense of commercial pollination services
- Over time, sustainable habitat provides a reliable, cost-effective source of pollinators
- Farmers with their own beekeeping operations can eliminate rental costs and potentially earn income by renting colonies to neighboring farmers

### Ecosystem Service Benefits and Cost Savings

Beyond direct revenue generation, pollinator farming delivers substantial economic benefits through ecosystem services and operational cost reductions:

#### Direct Cost Savings
- **Reduced hive rental costs**: Natural pollinator habitats can eliminate or reduce need for commercial pollination services
- **Lower pesticide costs**: Pollinator-friendly practices often align with reduced pesticide use
- **Decreased input dependency**: Less reliance on external inputs increases farm resilience

#### Yield and Quality Improvements
- **25-30% yield increases**: Fields with adequate pollination show substantial productivity gains
- **Better produce quality**: Larger, better-formed fruits and vegetables command higher market prices
- **Premium pricing opportunities**: Organic and pollinator-friendly certifications enable access to premium markets

#### Co-Benefits
- **Natural pest control**: Pollinator habitats support beneficial insects; studies show 75% reduction in aphid populations and 40% reduction of pest-induced crop damage near flower strips
- **Soil health**: Reduced erosion and improved water quality
- **Biodiversity enhancement**: Multiple ecosystem services beyond pollination
- **Climate resilience**: More diverse and resilient farming systems better adapted to environmental stresses

## Practical Implementation Strategies

### Farm Design and Habitat Integration

Successful integration of pollinator farming into vegetable operations begins with thoughtful farm design that accommodates pollinator needs while maintaining agricultural productivity.

#### Design Principles

1. **Continuous flowering resources**: Create a mix of flowering crops and trees that bloom across different seasons
2. **Marginal land utilization**: Convert unsuitable crop areas (poor drainage, soil quality issues) to pollinator habitat
3. **Strategic positioning**: Place habitats on south-facing slopes for optimal sun exposure
4. **Ecological networks**: Form habitats as part of connected landscape features

#### Key Habitat Elements

- **Field edges and corners**: Convert to pollinator resources through native plantings
- **Buffer zones**: Areas between different crop sections
- **Hedgerows and windbreaks**: Provide shelter and connectivity
- **Water sources**: Clean water close to apiaries, especially during hot months
- **Nesting sites**: Ground-nesting areas in marginal soils unsuitable for crops

#### Apiary Placement Considerations

- **Accessibility**: Locations reachable by truck or wheelbarrow in all weather
- **Spray drift protection**: Buffer from pesticide applications
- **Foraging distance**: Consider that honey bees travel 2+ miles for food
- **Neighbor coordination**: Work with adjacent farms to minimize pesticide exposure

### Crop Selection and Rotation Planning

Aligning crop selection and rotation with pollinator needs creates synergies that benefit both vegetable production and pollinator populations.

#### Pollinator-Friendly Crop Strategies

**Cover crops for pollinators:**
- Legumes (vetch, clover)
- Scorpion weed
- Buckwheat
- Phacelia
- Annual clovers with different blooming dates

**Intercropping systems:**
- Flowering plants next to primary crops
- Tall and short crop polycultures (corn and beans)
- Strips of pollinator-attracting plants within fields
- Flowering legumes (alfalfa, clover) allowed to bloom before harvest

#### Temporal Planning

- Select plants that bloom before and after cash crops flower
- Create continuous bloom throughout growing season (spring through late autumn)
- Plan annual rotations with pollinator temporal requirements in mind
- Ensure year-round support systems rather than brief seasonal food pulses

### Pesticide Management and IPM Integration

Managing pesticide use to protect pollinators represents one of the most critical aspects of pollinator-friendly farming.

#### Integrated Pest Management (IPM) Strategies

**Monitoring and scouting:**
- Regular field scouting to assess actual pest populations
- Use of economic thresholds to determine if treatment is necessary
- Trapping systems to monitor pest populations and time applications precisely
- Skip or delay applications when pest pressure is below threshold

**Pesticide application best practices:**
- Avoid applications during bloom periods when bees are actively foraging
- Select pesticides with lower toxicity to bees and shorter residual activity
- Implement spray drift management through buffer zones
- Communicate with neighboring growers about managed bee presence
- Time applications to avoid active foraging hours

**Organic and biological controls:**
- Use only approved biological and botanical pesticides
- Promote beneficial insect populations through habitat
- Employ physical barriers and cultural controls
- Rotate pest management tactics to reduce resistance

## Challenges and Barriers to Adoption

### Economic and Financial Constraints

Economic barriers represent significant obstacles to pollinator farming adoption for many vegetable farmers.

#### Upfront Investment Requirements

**Beekeeping equipment:**
- Hives: $80-$100 per package for commercial beekeepers; $200-$250 per hive
- Protective gear and tools
- Honey extraction equipment
- Storage solutions
- Processing facilities for commercial operations

**Habitat establishment:**
- Seeds or plants for pollinator plantings
- Site preparation costs
- Foregone agricultural production on dedicated land
- Maintenance equipment and labor

#### Delayed Returns

- Pollinator habitat benefits may take several years to fully materialize
- Gap between investment and return discourages adoption
- Farmers with lower pollination-dependent crops perceive little direct gain
- Short-term financial pressures conflict with long-term benefits

#### Access to Financial Support

- Uneven awareness of and access to government programs
- Certification processes require investment and regulatory navigation
- Small-scale and beginning farmers lack financial reserves
- Limited access to credit for non-traditional agricultural diversification
- Underdeveloped market infrastructure for bee products in some regions

### Technical and Management Challenges

The technical complexity of managing both vegetable production and pollinator populations presents substantial challenges requiring new skills and knowledge.

#### Colony Management Complexity

**Common challenges:**
- Difficulties catching and colonizing bees in new hives
- Theft and vandalism of equipment
- Insufficient bee forage in surrounding landscapes
- Drought impacts on nectar availability
- Pest management (Varroa mites, wax moths, predatory birds)
- Disease identification and treatment
- Queen management and swarm prevention

**System management:**
- Host-pathogen relationships requiring constant vigilance
- Climate variability affecting bee foraging and colony health
- Organic systems limiting treatment options
- Need for eco-friendly pest management alternatives

#### Labor and Time Constraints

- Beekeeping is labor-intensive throughout the season
- Peak beekeeping activities coincide with intensive vegetable production periods
- Modern commercial operations require more labor per hive than historically
- Tasks include: mite treatments, feeding, queen management, honey harvesting, dead colony removal
- Temporal overlap strains available management capacity
- Requires expanding workforce or accepting tradeoffs in management intensity

### Knowledge Gaps and Awareness Barriers

Limited awareness of pollinators' agricultural importance and economic value hinders adoption of pollinator farming practices.

#### Understanding Gaps

- Many farmers think of bees primarily as honey producers, overlooking pollination services
- Disconnect between pollinator presence and agricultural benefits
- Limited knowledge about pollinator ecology and habitat requirements
- Undervaluation of pollination services in farm economics
- Complexity of pollinator-crop relationships makes outcome prediction difficult

#### Information Delivery Challenges

- Local agricultural officers often lack technical skills in beekeeping
- Extension services have inadequate capacity for pollinator management support
- Information overload from multiple agricultural priorities
- Barriers including lack of financial and advisory support
- Insufficient local knowledge about context-specific practices
- Communication systems for integrated approaches still developing

#### Epistemic Barriers

- Differences between experiential farmer knowledge and scientific understanding
- Structural barriers limiting beekeeper participation in agricultural policy
- Difficulty integrating diverse knowledge systems into decision-making
- Limited farmer voice in research priority-setting

## Case Studies and Success Stories

### Small-Scale Integration Examples

#### Cory Klehm's Farm - United States

Operating a 24-acre vegetable farm, Cory Klehm successfully integrated beekeeping and pollinator-friendly practices:

**Approach:**
- Initially focused on soil health through cover crops and conservation tillage
- Developed cover crop mixture with differing blooming dates
- Combined fall-planted annual clovers with spring-planted phacelia, buckwheat, and sunflowers
- Created continuous bloom throughout the season

**Results:**
- Bees remained on farm rather than traveling miles for food
- Approximately 30% increase in crop yields
- Tomatoes showed particularly dramatic benefits
- Improved bee colony health

**Key lesson:** "The key to building a beneficial habitat for bees is to provide a constant food source which can be achieved by using a cover crop mixture with differing blooming dates so there was constantly something in bloom."

#### Integrated Farming - Jhajjar District, Haryana, India

A farmer in Haryana successfully combined beekeeping with horticultural crops and cereal crops:

**System characteristics:**
- Diversified operation with multiple agricultural components
- Beekeeping integrated with multi-crop system
- Ensured continuous flowering resources year-round

**Benefits:**
- Direct income through honey production
- Indirect benefits through enhanced pollination
- Agricultural diversification providing income and employment opportunities
- Synergistic interactions among system components

#### Women Farmers - Mikuri Village, Bhutan

Women farmers in Bhutan integrated beekeeping into existing agricultural activities following training:

**Context:**
- Five-rai integrated farming land growing coffee, cucumber, chili, and tropical fruits
- Challenges of water scarcity and agricultural productivity
- Week-long beekeeping training program

**Outcomes:**
- Recognition of beekeeping as additional income source
- Understanding of environmental benefits
- Skills in hive management, colony care, and honey harvesting
- Favorable positioning due to climatic conditions and organic product demand

**Participant feedback:** "Firstly, it benefits our natural environment. Secondly, I learned how to earn income through honey production and its management."

### Regional and Community-Level Initiatives

#### African Honeybee Initiative - South Africa

Large-scale community program integrating beekeeping with crop farming and forest conservation in Amathole district:

**Program design:**
- Promoted beekeeping in rural communities throughout forested areas
- Aimed to support rural households in developing honey micro-businesses
- Focused on reducing wild fires initiated by hunters of wild bee hives

**Results:**
- Increased incomes for newly trained and active beekeepers
- One beekeeper reported: "In previous years when I made measurements it was 15% increase in crop yield and this year when I made measurements it was 35%"
- Wild fires attributed to honey hunters more than halved
- Conservation co-benefits alongside livelihood improvements

**Challenges encountered:**
- Variable success rates and honey yields
- Theft, vandalism, and equipment loss
- Insufficient forage in some areas
- Ongoing pest management issues
- Need for continuous training and extension services

#### Coffee++ Thailand Project

Integration of beekeeping training into agricultural development program for smallholder farmers:

**Approach:**
- Capacity-building training targeting smallholder farmers, particularly women
- Focus on sustainable beekeeping as part of regenerative agriculture
- Each farmer manages five-rai plot facing soil degradation and water scarcity

**Objectives:**
- Revive biodiversity while boosting livelihoods
- Promote female empowerment in agricultural sector
- Establish beekeeping groups for knowledge sharing
- Create sustainable livelihood options working in harmony with environment

**Project coordinator insight:** "We encourage farmers to improve not only income and biodiversity in coffee farm by introducing beekeeping for more sustainable and productive agricultural system. The activity also promotes female farmers to set up beekeeping group for strengthening female empowerment and their role in the agricultural sector."

#### AGRI4POL Project - Europe

Large-scale, multi-country research and implementation initiative assisting transition toward pollinator-friendly agriculture:

**Research focus:**
- Evaluates genetic diversity of crop floral traits linked to pollinator attraction
- Studies how crop types and diversity affect pollination services
- Identifies strategies to optimize landscapes for crop pollination and biodiversity
- Investigates responses to future climate and land-use changes

**Implementation approach:**
- Multi-actor communities exchanging scientific, agronomic, economic, and policy knowledge
- Co-development with end-users to address practical needs
- Multiple European countries participating
- Integration along agri-food value chains

**Outcomes:**
- Evidence-based recommendations for landscape optimization
- Identification of co-benefits and trade-offs
- Building acceptance among farmers and society
- Supporting policy development at EU and national levels

## Future Trends and Innovations

### Technological Advancements in Pollinator Management

Emerging technologies are transforming pollinator management practices and creating new possibilities for integration with modern farming systems.

#### Precision Monitoring and AI

**Current innovations:**
- **BEEKIND platform**: AI-powered system providing hyperlocal data on climate, crop trends, and pollination patterns
- **Smart sensors**: Real-time monitoring of plant health triggering automated actions when flowers reach optimal receptivity
- **Colony health monitoring**: Advanced sensors tracking bee foraging patterns, colony health indicators, and environmental conditions
- **Early warning systems**: Providing alerts for potential problems enabling adaptive management

#### Precision Agriculture Integration

**Tools and applications:**
- **GIS mapping**: Combined with pollinator distribution data and landscape ecology models
- **Optimal habitat placement**: Identifying best locations for habitat creation
- **Landscape-level assessment**: Evaluating pollinator resources across farm and surrounding areas
- **Drone technology**: Aerial surveying of habitat quality, flower abundance monitoring, landscape connectivity assessment

#### Breeding Innovations

**Crop breeding:**
- Evaluation of genetic diversity for pollinator attraction traits
- Development of pollinator-smart crop varieties
- Enhanced nectar production
- Flower structures optimized for specific pollinator groups
- Extended flowering periods
- Incorporation of pollination traits alongside traditional breeding targets

**Pollinator breeding:**
- Disease-resistant honey bees and bumble bees
- Improved foraging efficiency
- Better adaptation to specific climatic conditions
- Enhanced effectiveness for particular crop systems

### Robotic and Automated Pollination Systems

Robotic pollination technologies are emerging as supplementary solutions addressing pollinator scarcity in specific contexts.

#### Current Capabilities

**Controlled environment applications:**
- 93.1% detection accuracy for individual small flowers
- 76.9% successful pollination rates for strawberry production
- Multi-armed systems for vertical farms providing parallelized pollination
- Effective in greenhouses where natural pollinators struggle

**Field applications:**
- Tractor-mounted systems delivering electrostatically charged dry pollen (e.g., Power Pollen for maize)
- Drone-based pollen redistribution trials
- Successful prototypes for kiwifruit pollination at commercial speeds
- Swarm robotic systems with improved collective control

#### Limitations and Role

**Current constraints:**
- High capital costs limiting accessibility
- Technical expertise required for operation and maintenance
- Environmental challenges (wind, rain, complex plant structures)
- Works better for self-pollinating crops than cross-pollination
- Cannot replicate ecosystem services beyond crop pollination

**Future integration:**
- Hybrid approaches combining biological and robotic systems
- Robotic supplementation triggered when natural pollination falls below thresholds
- Technology as backup rather than replacement for biological pollinators
- Emphasis remains on supporting healthy biological pollinator populations

### Regenerative Agriculture and Agroecological Integration

- Global animal pollination contributes $235-577 billion annually to agriculture

- Yield increases of 25-30% documented with adequate pollinator populations
- Multiple income streams create economic resilience

**Proven success across contexts:**

- Success demonstrated across diverse scales, crops, and geographical locations

### Future Outlook

The integration of pollinator farming into vegetable production aligns with broader transformations toward regenerative, agroecological, and climate-smart agriculture. As awareness grows of the essential connections between pollinators, food security, ecosystem health, and rural livelihoods, pollinator-friendly practices are transitioning from niche activities to mainstream agricultural requirements.

Technological innovations in precision monitoring, AI-driven decision support, and robotic supplementation will complement but not replace biological pollinators, emphasizing the continued centrality of supporting healthy, diverse pollinator populations through habitat and management interventions.

**The future of farming necessarily includes farming for pollinators**—not as an optional add-on but as a core component of productive, profitable, and sustainable agricultural systems that nourish both people and the planet. Vegetable farmers who embrace this integration position themselves at the forefront of agricultural innovation, contributing to solutions for pressing challenges facing global food systems while building resilient, diversified operations capable of thriving in an uncertain future.
